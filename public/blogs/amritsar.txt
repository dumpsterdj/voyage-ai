INTRO:
I arrived in Amritsar at 5 AM, bleary-eyed from an overnight train, and walked straight into one of the most powerful moments of my travelling life. The Golden Temple doesn't ease you in — it hits you all at once. The reflection of gold on still water. The low hum of Gurbani recitation floating across the complex. Thousands of people, silent and purposeful, moving toward something that seemed to matter to them completely.

I hadn't planned to cry. I did anyway.
---
SECTION: Day 1 Morning — The Golden Temple at Dawn
Arrive before 5:30 AM. I cannot stress this enough.

The Langar (community kitchen) starts at 4:30 AM and by the time you've washed your feet and walked across the causeway to the Harmandir Sahib, the morning light is doing something extraordinary to the gold.

The Palki ceremony — where the Guru Granth Sahib is ceremonially carried to the temple — happens at sunrise. Position yourself on the left side of the causeway for the best view.

- Cover your head (scarves available free at entrance)
- Remove shoes at the shoe counter (free, tip optional)
- Carry a small towel — the marble floor around the Sarovar is perpetually wet
---
SECTION: Day 1 Afternoon — Hall Bazaar & The Real Amritsar
Amritsar's soul lives in its lanes. Hall Bazaar is the main artery, but duck into the side streets and you'll find the city as it actually is.

What to look for:
- **Phulkari embroidery** — traditional Punjab folk art in vivid oranges and pinks
- **Amritsari Juttis** — handmade leather shoes, ₹400–800 from lanes near the temple
- **Wadiyan stalls** where women make sun-dried lentil crackers outside their homes

Stop at **Kanha Sweets** on Lawrence Road for Amritsari pinni — a dense, ghee-rich sweet made with wheat flour and jaggery. One piece is a meal.
---
SECTION: Day 1 Dinner — The One Restaurant You Cannot Miss
**Bharawan Da Dhaba**. Maharajpur Road. Get there by 7 PM or queue.

This place has been serving since 1912 and the dal makhani has been simmering for what feels like the entire century. Order:
- Dal Makhani (mandatory)
- Amritsari Fish — river sole in a cumin-forward batter, fried in mustard oil
- Makki di Roti with Sarson da Saag (November–February only)
- Lassi to finish — thick, cold, steel glass

Total for two: ₹400–500. Chaos is part of the experience.
---
SECTION: Day 2 — Jallianwala Bagh & Wagah Border
**Jallianwala Bagh** — ten minutes from the Golden Temple. The bullet holes in the walls from the 1919 massacre are still visible. Walk slowly. It's not a comfortable place. It shouldn't be.

**Wagah Border Ceremony** — book an auto-rickshaw (₹200–250 return). The Beating Retreat ceremony at sunset is pure theatrical nationalism. Whatever your politics, it's unlike anything else on earth. Arrive 90 minutes early for a good seat.
---
CLOSING:
Amritsar is one of those cities that gives you something you didn't know you needed. I came for the architecture and left with something quieter — a sense that hospitality, at its deepest level, isn't about hotels or service. It's about making a stranger feel that they belong.

The Langar feeds 100,000 people every single day. Free. No questions asked.

That's worth a 5 AM alarm.
