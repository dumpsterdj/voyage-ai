INTRO:
Nobody warns you about the sound.

Before you see the Ganges in Rishikesh, you hear it — a constant, low roar that fills the valley like the earth itself is breathing. I arrived on a Tuesday evening with a backpack and no real plan, walked down to Laxman Jhula bridge, and stood there for twenty minutes just listening.

Rishikesh is many things — yoga capital of the world, white-water rafting hub, pilgrimage town, backpacker hangout. Somehow it holds all of these identities at once without any of them feeling fake.
---
SECTION: Getting There & First Impressions
The easiest route from Delhi is by train to **Haridwar** (4–5 hours, ₹200–500) followed by a shared taxi or bus to Rishikesh (45 minutes, ₹80–150). The Shatabdi Express is the fastest option — book 2 weeks in advance on IRCTC.

Fly into Jolly Grant Airport if you're coming from further away. It's 35km from town and taxis charge ₹600–800.

First thing to know: Rishikesh is split into two areas.
- **Tapovan / Laxman Jhula** — the backpacker side. Cafes, guesthouses, yoga studios, rooftop restaurants overlooking the river.
- **Ramjhula / Muni Ki Reti** — slightly calmer, more local, better for walking the ghats in the morning.

Stay on the Laxman Jhula side for your first visit. The energy is infectious.
---
SECTION: White Water Rafting — The Real Reason People Come
The Ganges rafting stretch from **Shivpuri to Rishikesh** (16km) is one of the best beginner-to-intermediate rafting runs in Asia. Grades II to IV, takes about 2.5 hours, and the rapids have brilliant names — Golf Course, Double Trouble, Roller Coaster.

Operators: Book directly at the ghat the evening before. Standard price is **₹600–800 per person** including life jacket, helmet, and paddle. Avoid anyone charging under ₹500 — corners get cut on safety equipment.

- Go in the **morning** (7–11 AM) — calmer water, better light for photos, less crowded
- The **35km run from Kaudiyala** is for experienced rafters or adrenaline seekers — Grade IV-V rapids
- **Camping on the riverbank** overnight (₹1,500–2,500 all-in) is genuinely one of the best sleeps you'll ever have — bonfire, stars, river sound

> Avoid the monsoon season (July–August). The river is dangerous and rafting is banned.

SECTION: Yoga & Wellness — Take It Seriously or Don't Bother
Rishikesh has hundreds of yoga schools. Most of the ones targeting tourists are fine for a single drop-in class (₹300–500). But if you're here for the real thing, do some research.

**Parmarth Niketan** on the Ramjhula side is the most respected ashram. The **Ganga Aarti** held here every evening at sunset is extraordinary — fire, bells, chanting, and the river lit orange. Free to attend. Arrive 20 minutes early for a good spot on the ghat steps.

For serious practitioners: **Sivananda Ashram** and **Bihar School of Yoga** run structured residential programs (2 weeks minimum, very affordable). These are not spa retreats — they're the real thing, with 5 AM wake-ups and strict schedules.

Drop-in yoga recommendations:
- **Omkarananda Ganga Sadan** — small, serious, ₹300 per class
- **Yoga Niketan** — good for beginners, flexible timing

SECTION: Food — What to Eat & Where
Rishikesh is **100% vegetarian** (alcohol-free too, technically — though it flows freely in certain spots). This is not a limitation. The food is excellent.

**Little Buddha Cafe** on the Laxman Jhula bridge side — rooftop seating over the river, great thali, famous banana lassi. Budget ₹200–300 per person.

**Chotiwala Restaurant** — the most famous restaurant in town, two branches facing each other across the lane with identical names and a long-running rivalry. The food is similar; go to whichever has the shorter queue.

**German Bakery** (multiple locations) — for when you need a break from dal and rice. Proper coffee, banana bread, good pancakes. Backpacker staple.

**Tip:** The rooftop cafes on the far side of Laxman Jhula with river views are tourist-priced but worth it for breakfast with the Ganges below you. Budget ₹150–250.
---
SECTION: The Ghats at Dawn — Do Not Skip This
Set an alarm for 5:30 AM on at least one morning.

Walk to **Triveni Ghat** before sunrise. Watch the priests begin the morning prayers. Watch local women carry flower offerings to the water. Watch the light come up over the hills and turn the river from grey to silver to gold.

This is free. This costs nothing. This is the best thing you'll do in Rishikesh.

The evening **Ganga Aarti at Triveni Ghat** (7 PM, daily) is the other unmissable event — smaller and more local than the Parmarth version, which makes it feel more genuine.
---
CLOSING:
I stayed three days and it wasn't enough. Rishikesh has a way of slowing you down — the river does it, the mountains do it, the absence of car horns (the old town lanes are pedestrian-only) does it.

By day two I'd stopped checking my phone. By day three I was genuinely considering a two-week yoga course and a river-view room for the month.

I didn't stay. But I'm going back.
